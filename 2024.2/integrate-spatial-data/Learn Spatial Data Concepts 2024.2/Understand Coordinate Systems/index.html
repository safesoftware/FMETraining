What is a Coordinate?

A game of battleship. If I were to guess the coordinates B1, I would get a Hit, marked in green. If I said the Coordinates E6 (red), I would miss. (Wikimedia, CC-BY-SA)

Have you ever played Battleship? It’s a classic game that utilizes a Cartesian coordinate system as a part of its mechanics.

Built on a grid system with letters representing a location on the horizontal axis and numbers a location on the vertical axis, the game involves somewhat futile guessing using a combination of a letter and a number to mark a specific location on the grid. You would hope to “hit” your opponent’s hidden ship at this specific location.

This location is a coordinate: a specific point on a line, scale, or surface.

When it comes to maps, coordinates are numeric values used to represent specific points on the Earth. These are often communicated in pairs — a horizontal X value and a vertical Y value like 49.178010°, -122.842655° (aka. latitude and longitude).

Coordinates are an instrumental piece of data in a variety of industries. Surveyors need to know the exact points on the Earth at which to build, utilities need to be able to place networks in specific locations underground where they won’t be disturbed, and telecommunication services need to know precisely where to place service towers to support their clients. Without coordinates, life would be pretty chaotic (uncoordinated, one could say).

However, without any context or rules, these are just numbers. They could be anywhere or mean anything! It’s like they lack some system to define them!

What is a Coordinate System?

English, a sometimes nonsensical language, also allows you to use the word coordinate as a verb defined as “to bring into a common action, movement, or condition.” Conveniently, a coordinate system does just this.

A coordinate system defines a standard set of conditions or rules that apply to all its coordinates. You could say, "A coordinate system systematically coordinates coordinates.”

But it’s not always straightforward. The Earth is a complex shape, not a smooth and perfect sphere, as I experienced with the classroom globe. It’s more like a lump of playdough shaped into a ball by a toddler's tiny, clumsy fingers. Mountains, valleys, plains, fjords, canyons, and so much water. To top it off, it’s not static. The moon's gravity acts on the oceans, tectonic plates continue their slo-mo movements of nudging continents around, and even the poles shift.

So you can imagine, when people looked at this problem of locating places on the crazy-shaped, always-changing Earth, they had a lot of different things to consider. Some looked at specific regions, like a province, while others created systems oriented around tidal levels. Others ignored both of these things and chose something else entirely.

This is why we have thousands of geographic coordinate systems—there are many right answers depending on what you need. There is no single perfect coordinate system that is appropriate for all use cases.

These thousands of coordinate systems can co-exist because they are each defined by a standard set of rules or properties. At a high level, geographic coordinate systems all have:

  • A datum – remember, the Earth is a playdough sphere. A datum is the mathematical frame of reference that approximates the 3D shape of the Earth for measurement. This can be a perfect sphere like a classroom globe or ellipsoidal and flattened and is often shifted off the Earth's center of mass to provide more accurate measurements.
  • Angular units – The measurement used to describe the angle of a point on the Earth’s surface about the center of the Earth. The most common angular units used are decimal degrees (DD), degree minutes seconds (DMS), and decimal minutes (DM).
  • Prime Meridian – the north-south line along the Earth’s surface indicates 0° longitude. The most commonly used prime meridian runs through Greenwich, England.

These standardized models of points on the earth are key for ensuring accurate spatial data.
